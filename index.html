Transparency

Drawn on 2 data sources: International Development Statistics provided by OECD on commitments, disbursements, and official flows and own inquiries on

  • Permanent international staff
    • Professional and support staff
    • Nationals of industrialized and developing countries
    • Staff employed at headquarter and field offices
  • Consultants
  • Local staff
  • Total admin expenses
  • Salaries and benefits expenses
  • Total amount of development assistance disbursed

One point each detail if available online, half if provided after inquiring.

Specialization

The degree to which aid is not fragmented among too many donors, too many countries, and too many sectors as measured by Herfindahl Coefficients.

  • Probability 2 randomly selected aid dolllars will be
    • from the same donor for all net official development assistance: 9.6%
    • to the same country for any given donor: 4.6%
    • to the same sector for any given donor: 8.6%
    • from the same donor to the same country for the same sector: 1 in 2658
  • Fragmentation is extreme for even the smallest agencies

Selectivity

The extent to which aid avoids corrupt autocrats and goes to the poorest countries.

  • Share of total aid going to "unfree" and "unfree + part free": 30% and 80% respectively.
  • How much aid goes to corrupt countries.
  • Share of aid going to different income groups.
  • Composite score = .25 x Percentile Rank(Share not going to corrupt countries) + .25 x Percentile Rank(Share going to free countries) + .5 x Percentile Rank(Share going to low-income countries)

Effective Aid Channel

Extent to which aid is tied to political objectives or consists of food aid or technical assistance.

  • Focus on bilateral agencies.
  • Respective shares: 21%, 4, and 24. Considerable diversity.
  • 0.42 correlation between a country's share of techical assistance and share of tied aid.
  • Most highly ranked agencies: Switzerland, Ireland, Norway, Sweeden.
  • Lowest ranked agencies: Greece, Australia, United States.

Overhead Costs

Measures an agency's administrative costs relative to the amount of aid it gives using 2 indicators.

  • Ratio of costs to official development financing
    • entire administrative budget
    • wage and salaries
  • Official Development financing per employee
    • total agency employment
    • permanent internationally recruited staff

Case study

Millennium Villages Project

  • Solution to poverty trap.
  • $120 million invested in 14 villages throughout sub-Saharan Africa.
  • Proof of concept to be scaled up.
  • Solution adapted to different agro-economic zone to prove blue-print can be used anywhere with modification.
  • Dertu turned from nomadic village into a slum.
  • Certain improvement, but unclear direct impact.

Deworm the World

  • 4 years trial of textbooks distribution, followed by 3 years of deworming.
  • Improvement of health, education, and salaries.
  • 60 to 1 ratio in improvement of lifetime wages and cost of treatment.
  • Adopted across Africa and India.
  • British Medical Journal reports no effect on weight, height, school attendance, or school performance.

The Problems of Overhead

  • Convenient for comparing agencies, requires no actual work.
  • Small agency vs big agency.
  • Discourages transparency.
  • Disparity between approaches to countries and to agencies.
“Great minds discuss ideas; average minds discuss events; small minds hang on to ‘overhead’ as a way to determine aid agencies' effectiveness.”
